How to make a great journalism story lead

Assembly time: 10 minutes
Usefulness rating: 100%

Writers of news stories generally utilize a special style and format in crafting their opening lead. Pay attention to each step on how to create a great story lead!

  • Things You'll Need

  • Pen
  • Paper
  • Computer
  • Focus
  • Intelligence
  • Creativity

  1. Know a "lead" from a "lede."

    The use of "lede" dates back to when newspaper publishers needed to differentiate between the term used for the beginning of a news story and the molten lead used in the printing process.

    The invention of hot metal typesetting came in the 1880's, so it was important for people in the journalism communitity to differentiate between molten lead and a "lead".

  2. Lead with your best stuff.

    A news story lead is based on the premise that you need to save the best for first. It has to catch the reader's eye.

  3. Worry about the lead, not the conclusion.

    Unlike essays or books or most other forms of writing, news articles are written on the assumption that the reader may not finish the piece.

  4. Provide the 5 "W's" and "H."

    A traditional lead attempts to answer most or all of the key questions.

  5. Keep it short.

    While the exact numbers vary, journalism students are often taught to write first sentences of between 25 and 35 words, and not more than 40.

    The phrase "Once upon a time" is a typical hook in a novel that captures a reader's attention because it's short and sweet.

  6. Focus on action.

    Readers are drawn in by active sentences that propel them forward into the story.

  7. Break the rules when inspiration strikes.

    A writer is more artist than scientist, so quality is better than quantity.

  8. Consider the benefits of leads for all writing.

    What writer, from a high schooler with a book report due to a grizzled novelist, cannot benefit from learning leads?

    Writing for anyone is hard. The goal is to just keep practicing and watch yourself improve overtime.
